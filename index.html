LIFE

  Nectarios of Aegina (Greek: Άγιος Νεκτάριος Αιγίνης), born Anastasios Kephalas (Greek: Αναστάσιος Κεφαλάς) was the Metropolitan of Pentapolis. He was on 1 October 1846 in Selymbria (today Silivri, Istanbul) in the Ottoman Empire to a poor family. His parents, Dimos and Maria Kephalas, were pious Christians.

  At the age of 14, he moved to Constantinople (Istanbul) to work and further his education. In 1866, at age 20, he moved to the island of Chios to take a teaching post. On November 7, 1876, he became a monk, at age 30, in the Monastery of Nea Moni, for he had long wished to embrace the ascetic life.

 Three years after becoming a monk he was ordained a deacon, taking the name Nectarios. He graduated from the University of Athens in 1885. During his years as a student of the University of Athens he wrote many books, pamphlets, and Bible commentaries.

  Following his graduation he went to Alexandria, Egypt, where he was ordained a priest and served the Church of Saint Nicholas in Cairo. He was consecrated Metropolitan bishop of Pentapolis (an ancient diocese in Cyrenaica, in what is now Libya) by the Greek Orthodox Patriarch Sophronios in 1889.

  He served as a bishop in Cairo for one year. Nectarios was very popular with the people, which gave rise to jealousy among his colleagues. They were able to persuade his superior that Nectarios had ambitions to displace the Patriarch. Nectarios was suspended from his post without explanation. He then returned to Greece in 1891, and spent several years as a preacher (1891–1894). He was then director of the Rizarios Ecclesiastical School for the education of priests in Athens for fifteen years. He developed many courses of study, and wrote numerous books, while preaching widely throughout Athens.


 In 1904, at the request of several nuns, he established Holy Trinity Monastery for them on the island of Aegina. In December 1908, at the age of 62, Nectarios resigned from his post as school director and withdrew to the Holy Trinity Convent on Aegina, where he lived out the rest of his life as a monk. He wrote, published, preached, and heard confessions. He also tended the gardens, carried stones, and helped with the construction of the monastery buildings that were built with his own funds.

  Nectarios died on November 8, 1920, at the age of 74, following hospitalization for prostate cancer and two months of treatment. His body was taken to the Holy Trinity Convent, where he was buried by his best friend Saint Savvas of Kalymnos, who later painted the first icon of Nectarios. The funeral of Nectarios was attended by multitudes of people from all parts of Greece and Egypt. The relics of Nectarios were removed from the grave on 2 September 1953.

  He was officially recognized as a saint by the Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople in 1961. His feast day is celebrated every year on 9 November. He was also known as the Miracle Worker of Aegina.

  Among the songs sung on November 9 in his memory, which the Orthodox Church dedicated to Saint Nectarios, is the troparion (a short hymn or an apolytikion) in the First Tone, that speaks briefly about the saint's life.

  O faithful, let us honor Nectarios, divine servant of Christ, offspring of Silivria and guardian of Aegina, who in these latter years was manifested as the true friend of virtue. All manner of healing wells forth for those who in piety cry out, "Glory to Christ who glorified you; glory to Him who, through you, wrought wonders; glory to Him who, through you, works healing for all."

  A major film production, starring well-known foreign and Greek actors and actresses, will mark the 100th anniversary of the falling asleep in the Lord of Saint Nectarios, the most popular Saint of the Orthodox Church. The title of the film is "Man of God" and it will officially premiere in November 2020. It is about the biography of Saint Nectarios.

WRITINGS



Teachings

  Saint Metropolitan Nectarios of Pentapolis is one of the greatest theologians of the early twentieth century, with an exceptional level of academic training and a holy zeal for preaching from an early age. He wrote numerous works of significance and consistency that justify him with the words "you were crowned among the Holy Fathers". Thus in Greece the following works were published: "Historical Study on the Causes of Schism" (princeps 1911 edition, in 2 volumes); "Orthodox Catechism" (Athens 1899 princeps edition); "On the Immortality of the Soul and on the Holy Remembrances" (Athens, 1901); "The Ecumenical Synods of the Church of Christ. A Study of the Holy Icons" (reprinted at Thessaloniki in 1972), "Psaltirion" in lyrical meter and with explanatory notes (Athens, 1908); "Theotokarion - songs and hymns dedicated to the Most Holy Mother of God, Eternal Virgin Mary" (1907, Athens), published in at least 6 editions; "Christian Morality Courses for Students of the Rizareion School" (last edition in 1992 in Athens); "Know yourself"; "On the Holy Sacraments"; "Christology ..." "History of the Gospel"; "Historical Study of the Honest Cross"; "On the One, Holy, Catholic and Apostolic Church"; "About Repentance and Confession."

Poems

  Throughout the period of his episcopacy, Saint Nectarios spent much time in prayer and contemplation, and dedicated himself to the monastic life. His spiritual lifestyle, and his particular dedication to the Virgin Mary inspired him to write a wide variety of religious poetry, much of which was published during his life, and after his repose in 1920. One of the many poems he wrote is "Agni Parthene" (Greek: Ἁγνὴ Παρθένε) or "O Virgin Pure". According to a tradition passed down on the island of Aegina, St. Nectarios reportedly composed the text for this poem after having seen a vision of the Theotokos in a dream where she asked him to record this poem. The original script can still be viewed on his prayer table in his bedroom at this monastery. It was later published as a poetic hymn for non-liturgical use and private edification in his publication called "Theotokarion of Odes & Hymns for the Most-Holy Theotokos and Ever-Virgin Mary" (Θεοτοκάριον, ἤτοι προσευχητάριον μικρόν) of 1905, which included many other similar poems.

PILGRIMAGE

  If you want to go on a pilgrimage to the monastery built by St. Hierarch Nectarius on the island of Aegina you must arrive in Athens (Greece). From there you go by bus, subway or taxi to Dana 8 in the port of Piraeus, from where you take a ferry to Aegina (approximately cost 15 euros round trip). The ferry travels this route in about 1 hour. From the port of Aegina take the bus or taxi to the Monastery of St. Nectarios.
